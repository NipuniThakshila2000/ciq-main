This Is Pre-Decision Moral Foresight

Civic Conscience Sandbox

A government, NGO, company, ministry, or public institution can place a draft policy, speech, campaign, law, reform, or public message into the sandbox before launch. The AI then tests how that proposal may be morally interpreted by different ECC Profiles.

  • Will this build trust, or trigger mistrust?
  • Which communities may feel unheard, misrepresented, or violated?
  • Where are dignity, religious conscience, gender, shame, honour, safety, justice, or identity sensitivities?
  • How can the language be adjusted before harm is caused?
  • What kind of dialogue is needed before implementation?

The Sandbox is a listening architecture and rehearsal space where institutions can test decisions, simulate community responses, anticipate consequences, and refine policy before it becomes conflict.

ECC Agents

An ECC Agent is an AI-assisted model of a community's moral knowledge, built from consented, structured, and revisable community input.

It does not speak finally for a community or replace consultation. It gives a pre-dialogue signal so institutions can prepare to listen better.

ECC Agents do not replace communities. They help institutions prepare to listen better before real consultation begins.

CCC Orchestrator

The CCC Orchestrator compares multiple ECC Agents and identifies shared civic values, tensions, translation points, conflict risks, dialogue priorities, and policy redesign options.

Its central question is: where can communities remain distinct and still belong to one civic order?

The CCC Orchestrator helps identify where moral pluralism can be converted into common civic trust rather than fragmentation.

The Challenge

When Institutions Misread Community Conscience

A policy may be technically correct but fail because it ignores dignity, modesty, religious conscience, historical memory, gender sensitivity, institutional mistrust, or local concepts of fairness.

When institutions ignore how communities understand dignity, shame, honour, safety, fairness, justice, corruption, and trust, they create epistemic injustice.

Epistemic injustice occurs when a community's knowledge, testimony, experience, or moral language is dismissed, misunderstood, or excluded from decision-making.

The CIQ Civic Conscience Framework

From Community Conscience to Common Civic Trust

ECC Profile

An Epistemic Community Conscience Profile maps the moral knowledge of a community, culture, institution, faith group, profession, or civic population.

  • What does this community experience as dignity?
  • What does it experience as violation?
  • What does it regard as fair or unjust?
  • Who does it trust as a source of knowledge?
  • What institutional actions feel unsafe, dishonouring, or exclusionary?
  • What conditions are necessary for participation, trust, and belonging?

CCC Index

The Common Civic Conscience Index brings multiple ECC Profiles together to identify shared civic ground across communities. It does not erase difference or force all communities into one moral template.

  • Where communities already share civic values
  • Where moral language differs but can be translated
  • Where trust has broken down
  • Where institutional design is creating exclusion
  • Where justice claims overlap or conflict
  • Where dialogue, education, transparency, or process reform is needed

Example

Policy Sensitivity Begins With Moral Context

An epistemically just system asks what a community understands as dignity, modesty, safety, trust, and appropriate care before labeling behavior as non-compliance.

01

Gender-Sensitive Care

In some contexts, women may avoid clinics where male doctors or male nurses are predominant. A purely administrative system may call this low compliance, poor awareness, or lack of access. CIQ asks which dignity, modesty, safety, and trust conditions must exist for care to be accepted.

02

Religious Conscience

During the COVID-19 pandemic, Sri Lanka's mandatory cremation policy created deep distress among Muslim communities whose religious conscience required burial practices. The issue became a question of dignity, trust, religious recognition, and whether affected communities felt heard by the state.

Scenario-dependent ECC drift

The key insight: people shift under pressure

A stakeholder's civic conscience profile is not static. A group may appear highly pluralistic in normal discussion, but become less pluralistic when identity, dignity, sacred values, or safety feel threatened.

Scenario-dependent ECC drift shows how stakeholder responses change under different moral conditions.

Condition Pluralism Authority Identity Threat Fragmentation Risk
Normal public discussion High Medium Low Low
After sacred-value insult Low High High Severe
After inclusive consultation Medium-high Medium Medium Moderate
After trusted mediator intervention High Medium Low Low

This helps leaders see not only where conflict may emerge, but what conditions may reduce it.
